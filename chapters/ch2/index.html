Stocks & Flows

Every system has something that piles up — water in a bathtub, money in a savings account, carbon in the atmosphere. In System Dynamics, this is called a stock: anything you can measure at a single moment in time.

So what makes a stock change? Flows — the streams that fill it or drain it. The faucet is an inflow. The drain is an outflow. When more flows in than out, the stock rises. When more flows out, it falls. When they're equal, the stock holds steady — that's dynamic equilibrium.

Stocks and flows — that's it. This one idea is the foundation of every System Dynamics model. The equation below says the same thing, just in math:

$$\text{Stock}(t) = \text{Stock}(t_0) + \int_{t_0}^{t} [\text{Inflow}(s) - \text{Outflow}(s)] \, ds$$

Play with the faucet and drain below and watch the water level respond. Notice how the stock never jumps — it always changes gradually, because flows take time. This is why cutting a deficit doesn't fix debt overnight, and why populations keep growing even after birth rates drop.

Behavior Patterns

Every system tells a story over time. Plot any stock on a graph and you'll see one of a few recognizable shapes. These are behavior patterns — each one is a fingerprint of the feedback structure hiding underneath.

Learning these patterns is like learning to read an EKG. Once you spot the shape, you instantly know which feedback loop is in charge. Click each pattern below to see it in action.

System Traps: When Structure Creates Problems

Some feedback structures keep producing the same problems, over and over. These recurring patterns are called system traps — and they show up everywhere: economics, ecology, politics, business. Spotting the trap is the first step to breaking free.

Tragedy of the Commons

Some resources belong to nobody and everybody at once — a shared lake, the atmosphere, a common grazing field. Each person gets the full benefit of taking more, but the cost of overuse gets spread across everyone. That creates a dangerous setup: the 'rational' choice for each individual is to grab as much as possible.

And the result? When everyone follows that logic, the shared resource collapses — and everyone loses. This is the Tragedy of the Commons, one of the most powerful system traps. It's behind overfishing, carbon emissions, groundwater depletion, and antibiotic resistance.

Try it yourself: you decide how many fishing boats go out on a shared lake. More boats mean more profit — until the fish run out.

Tragedy of the Commons

Try it: Start with 20 boats (sustainable), then jump to 60 (overshoot), then 90 (collapse). The exact same pattern plays out with carbon emissions, groundwater pumping, and antibiotic overuse.

Escalation

When two sides keep reacting to each other's moves, a reinforcing loop of escalation kicks in. Country A boosts military spending. Country B matches it. So Country A spends even more. Each move makes perfect sense on its own — but the spiral is collectively insane.

You see the same pattern in price wars between companies, attack ads during elections, and outrage cycles on social media. Nobody wins, but everyone pays more.

Drift to Low Performance

This is the sneakiest trap — nobody sees it happening. When performance falls short, instead of pushing harder to close the gap, we quietly lower the goal to match reality. 'That's just how things are now.' Each little adjustment seems perfectly reasonable.

But over time, the goal and performance sink together — like the frog in slowly heating water that never notices the danger. You see this in crumbling infrastructure, schools that slowly get worse, and organizations that gradually accept 'good enough' as the new standard.

Set the erosion rate below. At 0%, the standard holds and performance bounces back. Crank it up, and watch both lines slide downhill together.

Success to the Successful

Winners get rewards that help them win again. A company with more market share can spend more on ads, which brings more customers, which funds more ads. The rich get richer — not because they're smarter, but because of a reinforcing loop of advantage.

Think of Monopoly: once one player pulls ahead, they buy more properties, collect more rent, and pull further ahead. The game is really over halfway through — you just don't know it yet. The same dynamic drives wealth inequality, platform monopolies, and algorithms that boost whatever's already popular.
