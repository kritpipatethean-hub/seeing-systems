Why SD for Economics?

In 2005, almost no mainstream economist saw the financial crisis coming. But a small group using System Dynamics did — because they weren't watching GDP or stock prices. They were watching the structure: how debt was piling up, how feedback loops were tightening, how the system was setting itself up to crash.

Chapter 2 gave you the language — stocks, flows, feedback loops, system traps. Now let's use it on the most complex system humans have ever built: the economy.

The Goodwin Growth Cycle

Why does the economy keep swinging between booms and busts? Not because of bad luck or random shocks — but because of its own feedback structure. Employment, wages, and profits chase each other in circles, like wolves and rabbits in a predator-prey dance.

The loop works like this: when jobs are plentiful, workers push for higher wages. Higher wages eat into profits. Lower profits mean less investment. Less investment means fewer jobs. Fewer jobs mean lower wages. And lower wages restore profits — starting the cycle over. This is the Goodwin Growth Cycle (1967), the simplest model that produces realistic business cycles.

Remember oscillation from Chapter 2? This is what it looks like in a real economy. Two stocks — the employment rate (λ) and workers' share of income (ω) — chase each other endlessly:

$$\frac{d\lambda}{dt} = \lambda \left[ \frac{\kappa(1-\omega)}{v} - (\alpha + \beta) \right]$$ $$\frac{d\omega}{dt} = \omega \left[ \Phi(\lambda) - \alpha \right]$$

Two things shape how dramatic this cycle gets. First: how sensitive wages are to the job market. In some economies, wages barely budge when unemployment drops — think of places with weak unions or rigid contracts. In others, wages skyrocket the moment workers gain bargaining power. The more sensitive wages are, the wilder the swings between boom and bust. Second: how fast productivity grows. When workers get more productive each year, the economy can absorb higher wages without killing profits — the whole equilibrium shifts. Slow productivity growth? The economy stagnates and cycles stay tight. Fast productivity growth? More room to grow, but the dynamics change in surprising ways.

Use the sliders to explore. Wage Sensitivity controls how aggressively wages respond to employment — crank it up and watch the swings go wild. Productivity Growth sets how fast the economy expands — higher values shift where the cycle settles.

Minsky's Financial Instability

Remember the bathtub? Debt works the same way. Borrowing fills it up. Repayment drains it. When you borrow more than you repay, year after year, the tub fills — slowly at first, then all at once.

Hyman Minsky (1992) saw that economies pass through three stages of borrowing. In the Hedge phase, firms earn enough to cover all their debts — things are safe. In the Speculative phase, they can pay the interest but not the principal — they need to keep rolling over loans. In the Ponzi phase, they can't even cover the interest — they have to borrow more just to stay alive. The chilling insight? Stability is what causes instability. When times are good, people borrow more, because why wouldn't they?

Let's write this down in the stock-flow language you already know. Debt (D) is a stock. It grows when firms invest (I) more than they earn in profits (Π):

$$\frac{dD}{dt} = I - \Pi$$

But the raw debt number doesn't tell you much — what matters is debt relative to the economy's size. Divide both sides by GDP (Y) and you get the debt ratio d = D/Y. This ratio has two enemies: when the interest rate (r) exceeds GDP growth (g), old debt snowballs faster than the economy can outgrow it. And when investment outpaces profits, firms keep piling on new debt:

$$\frac{dd}{dt} = \underbrace{d(r - g)}_{\text{old debt snowballs}} + \underbrace{\left(\frac{I}{Y} - \frac{\Pi}{Y}\right)}_{\text{new borrowing}}$$

When both terms are positive — high interest rates AND heavy borrowing — the debt ratio accelerates. That's exactly how Hedge economies slide into Speculative territory, and eventually into Ponzi collapse.

Set investor confidence (animal spirits — Keynes's term for the gut instinct that drives investment decisions beyond cold calculation) and the cost of borrowing (interest rate), then watch the economy slide through Minsky's three phases:

0.5 2 1

How confident investors feel about the future. Low (0.5) = cautious — firms only borrow when profits are strong, the economy stays in Hedge territory for decades. High (2) = euphoric optimism — everyone borrows aggressively chasing growth, the boom feels unstoppable... until debt explodes and triggers a Minsky Moment.

The cost of borrowing. Low (1%) = cheap money — debt grows slowly even with heavy borrowing, crisis takes decades to arrive. High (10%) = expensive money — interest payments eat into profits fast, pushing the economy from Hedge to Ponzi much sooner. This is the r in the equation: when r > g, old debt snowballs on its own.

Minsky Debt Cycle

Try it: Start with animal spirits at 1.0 and interest at 3% — the economy hums along for decades. Now push animal spirits to 1.8 — everyone's borrowing, the boom is glorious... until debt explodes and it all comes crashing down. Try 8% interest — the crisis arrives even faster. The 2008 crash wasn't a freak accident. It was a bathtub that nobody watched overflow.

Why SD for Economics?

Most economics textbooks assume markets find equilibrium — like studying a bathtub only when the water is perfectly still. System Dynamics says: the water is never still. Money flows in and out constantly, delays pile up, and feedback loops amplify every wobble.

Here's a tool that cuts through political spin: the Sectoral Balances Identity. Government balance + trade balance + private balance = zero. Always. This isn't economics — it's arithmetic. If the government runs a surplus and trade is balanced, then the private sector must be in deficit. There is literally no way around it.

Steve Keen (2013) discovered something surprising: it's not how much debt there is that predicts a crash — it's how fast debt is speeding up. This Credit Accelerator tracks closely with jobs and asset prices. When borrowing accelerates, the economy booms. When it decelerates, everything falls apart.

$$(G - T) + (X - M) + (S - I) = 0$$

Sectoral Balances & Credit Accelerator

Try it: Set government spending to 3% — the private sector ends up in surplus (good for households). Now slash it to -1% (austerity) — the private sector is forced into deficit, no matter what. Crank credit growth to 8% — jobs boom as borrowing accelerates, then crash when it reverses. The lesson: one sector's surplus is always another sector's deficit. Always.

Key Insight: System Dynamics sees what equilibrium economics misses. Debt accumulates. Confidence feeds on itself. Delays hide the danger until it's too late. The economy isn't a machine that finds balance — it's a living system, driven by the same reinforcing and balancing loops you learned in Chapter 2.

So far, we've modeled the economy from the top down — big-picture stocks and flows. But what if we zoomed in and modeled each worker, each firm, each bank making their own decisions? That's Agent-Based Modeling, and it's what Chapter 4 is all about.
