What is System Dynamics?

System Dynamics is a methodology for understanding how complex systems change over time. Founded by Jay W. Forrester at MIT in the 1950s, it uses computer simulation to reveal how the structure of a system—its feedback loops, delays, and nonlinearities—determines its behavior.

Unlike traditional approaches that break problems into isolated parts, System Dynamics looks at the whole system and how its parts interact to create patterns of behavior over time.

Key Insight: A system's behavior comes from its structure. Want different behavior? Change the structure — the feedback loops, the delays, the information flows.

In System Dynamics, we write this idea as a simple equation:

$$\text{System Behavior} = f(\text{Structure})$$

In plain English: what we see happening (growth, decline, oscillation) is a direct result of how the system is wired — its feedback loops, time delays, and nonlinear relationships. Rewire the structure, and the behavior changes.

Counterintuitive Behavior

Here's the biggest surprise from System Dynamics: complex systems often behave in ways that are counterintuitive. Forrester (1971) found that well-meaning policies designed to solve social problems frequently backfire.

Feedback Loops: The Engine of Every System

Every system runs on two types of feedback loops. Understand these two, and you can understand any system.

Reinforcing Loop (R) — Growth or Collapse

A reinforcing loop amplifies whatever is happening. More leads to more. Less leads to less. Picture a snowball rolling downhill — it picks up snow, gets bigger, rolls faster, picks up even more snow.

Example: Compound interest. More money → more interest earned → even more money. That's exponential growth in action.

Balancing Loop (B) — Pulling Back to Equilibrium

A balancing loop pushes back against change, pulling the system toward a target. It works like a thermostat — room too cold? Heater kicks on. Warm enough? It shuts off.

Example: City overcrowding. More people → housing gets tight → quality of life drops → people leave → fewer people. The system corrects itself.

Key Insight: Every system is a tug-of-war between reinforcing and balancing loops. Whichever loop dominates at any moment decides what happens — growth, stability, oscillation, or collapse.
