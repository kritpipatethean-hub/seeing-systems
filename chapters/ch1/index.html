What is System Dynamics?

System Dynamics is a methodology for understanding how complex systems change over time. Founded by Jay W. Forrester at MIT in the 1950s, it uses computer simulation to reveal how the structure of a system—its feedback loops, delays, and nonlinearities—determines its behavior.

Unlike traditional approaches that break problems into isolated parts, System Dynamics looks at the whole system and how its parts interact to create patterns of behavior over time.

Key Insight: The structure of a system determines its behavior. To change behavior, you must change the structure—the feedback loops, delays, and information flows.

$$\text{System Behavior} = f(\text{Structure: Feedback Loops, Delays, Nonlinearities})$$

Brief Timeline

1950s
Jay Forrester develops System Dynamics at MIT, initially for industrial management.
1961
"Industrial Dynamics" published — the first major SD textbook.
1969
"Urban Dynamics" applies SD to city growth and decay problems.
1972
"Limits to Growth" uses SD to model global resource depletion — becomes a worldwide bestseller.
2000
Sterman publishes "Business Dynamics" — now the definitive SD textbook used worldwide.
Today
SD is applied to public health, tobacco control, climate change, supply chains, and more.

Counterintuitive Behavior

One of the most important insights from System Dynamics is that complex systems often behave in ways that are counterintuitive. As Forrester (1971) observed, policies designed to fix social problems frequently make them worse.

Try the simulation below: you are managing a city's population. Use the sliders to set policies and see how the system responds—often in unexpected ways!

Population Simulation

Notice how increasing housing investment beyond a certain point can actually attract so many people that quality of life drops. This is the "fixes that fail" archetype — a common pattern in complex systems.

Mental Models & System Thinking

Every decision we make is based on mental models—our internal pictures of how the world works. System Dynamics makes these mental models explicit through diagrams and equations, allowing us to test our assumptions with simulation.
